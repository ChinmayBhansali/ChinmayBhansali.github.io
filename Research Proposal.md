Motivation

AI-assisted learning tools, from conversational tutors and automated feedback systems to adaptive dashboards, are increasingly mediating how students learn. These tools are not neutral: they make design decisions that shape how learners receive information, regulate their own progress, and develop mental models of the subject matter. Yet most of these tools are evaluated primarily on algorithmic performance, with relatively little attention to how the interface layer itself affects the learning experience.

This gap became concrete to me during a course-based HCI project at UBC, where an AI-built prototype introduced an interaction artifact that disrupted usability in ways our team failed to anticipate. The incident raised a broader question that I believe matters for AI-assisted learning: when AI systems make interface decisions autonomously, how do those decisions interact with the cognitive and behavioural processes that learning depends on? The interface is not just a delivery mechanism. It is an active participant in how learners engage, persist, and self-regulate.

---

Research Question

How do the interface design decisions embedded in AI-assisted learning tools affect learner engagement, cognitive load, and self-regulated learning behaviours, and what human-centred evaluation methods can be used to systematically assess and improve these interactions?

---

Proposed Workstreams

1. Characterize learner interaction with AI-assisted tools: Use HCI evaluation methods including think-aloud protocols (co-discovery might also be insightful?), interaction logging, and experience sampling to understand how learners actually engage with AI tutors, feedback systems, and learning dashboards, with particular attention to where the interface creates confusion, disengagement, or miscalibrated self-assessment.

2. Link interface factors to learning outcomes: Examine how specific interface-level variables, like feedback presentation, prompt design, navigation structure, response latency, correlate with measurable learning behaviours and outcomes.

3. Develop HCI-informed design guidelines: Translate findings into actionable evaluation frameworks and design principles that practitioners and researchers can apply when building or assessing AI-assisted learning interfaces.
