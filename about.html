Where I Come From

Born and raised in India

I grew up in Rajasthan, India, in multiple schools where electronics were banned entirely. No laptops, no phones, just notebooks and the long, supervised quiet of a system that had very clear ideas about what a good student looked like. I was that student. But some part of me kept leaking through the rules.

When my friends and I wanted to play games and had nothing to play them on, I didn't feel the restriction as a wall. I felt it as an invitation. I designed a Pokémon RPG on notebook paper: hand-drawn maps, turn-based battles tracked in pencil, rules written in the margins. My friends played it and got genuinely lost in it. I remember watching them more than I played it myself, watching the way their faces changed when the story surprised them, wondering what had just passed between my mind and theirs through nothing but paper. I didn't have a name for what that was. I just knew I needed to keep doing it.

The Journey

From India to UBC

I arrived in Canada at seventeen with a scholarship, two suitcases, and no laptop. A store employee sold me one I thought I understood. Two years later, when it no longer suited my needs, I started researching why and fell into something unexpected: the discovery that people at companies like Apple and Microsoft make deliberate, considered choices about who their users are and what those users need. Design is not decoration. Someone, somewhere, is deciding how millions of people experience a machine, and sometimes those decisions get made without the actual people in mind at all.

That was the first time I understood what I wanted to study, not because it was a smart career move, but because it was the same question I had been living since the notebook RPG: what passes between a mind and a system, and how do we make that passage more honest? Human-Computer Interaction gave my oldest curiosity its first real name. UBC's Integrated Sciences program let me pursue it properly, combining Computer Science, Psychology, and Mathematics, not as separate subjects, but as three lenses trained on the same problem.

Why UX Research

Why research, not design?

The honest answer is that I find the why more interesting than the what.

Design asks: what should this look like, and how should it work? Research asks: what do people actually need, what assumptions are baked into this system, and where does the gap between the two live? The second set of questions is the one I keep coming back to.

During the Food Manager project, our team built what we thought was an efficient prototype, and users rated the recipe search feature as fast and easy. However, our click-count data told a completely different story: it was the least efficient task flow in the entire app. That contradiction isn't a design problem. It's a research problem. It's a question about why people's felt experience diverges from their actual behavior, and what that gap means for how we build things. Uncovering that kind of discrepancy, and figuring out what to do with it, is where I want to spend my energy.

I care about understanding people more than I care about making things look good. That's not a knock on design. Good design matters enormously. But the foundation it rests on is research, and that foundation is where I want to work.

Skills & Methods

What I bring to the table

I've learned these methods by doing them, not just studying them. In CPSC 344, I ran formative fieldwork with semi-structured interviews, built affinity diagrams to surface patterns I hadn't expected, and led a mixed-methods summative evaluation that revealed findings our team hadn't anticipated. At the SPIN Lab, I'm co-designing user studies for research on technology-mediated social touch: developing protocols, building psychometric questionnaires, and preparing to analyze movement trajectory and physiological data. Each project has taught me that rigor and empathy aren't in tension. The most careful research is also the most human.

Beyond Research

The human side

Both of these things, making music with other people and paying attention to the world around me, keep reminding me that most of what matters in an experience happens beneath the surface. That's exactly what I'm trying to understand in my research.

Looking Ahead

What's next?

I'm graduating in 2026, and I'm genuinely open about what comes next. Whether that's a PhD in HCI or a UX research role in the industry, the through-line is the same. I want to work on problems where the gap between what a system assumes about people and what people actually are is costing someone something real. I want to do that work carefully, honestly, and in collaboration with people who take it seriously.
