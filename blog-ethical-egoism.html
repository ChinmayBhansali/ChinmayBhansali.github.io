Is Self-Interest Really Natural? Against Ethical Egoism

Challenging the premise that self-interest is humanity's default with evolutionary and neuroscience evidence.

January 27, 2026 Ethics Altruism
Prompt: Proponents of ethical egoism often believe it's most natural for people to act in their own self-interest. Argue for or against this premise.

Proponents of ethical egoism often assume that it is most natural for people to act in their own self-interest. But is this really the truth, or just a convenient simplification of human behavior? Frans de Waal, in his influential research "Putting the altruism back into altruism", makes compelling points about human cooperation that directly challenge ethical egoism's core assumptions.

I argue that proponents of ethical egoism fundamentally misunderstand human nature when they claim self-interest is our default setting.

Recent evolutionary research reveals a different picture. The PNAS study on altruism evolution found that cooperation emerged across 43 different evolutionary models, suggesting altruistic behavior has deep biological roots. Fletcher and Zwick's research demonstrates that cooperation can evolve even without genetic similarity between helpers, indicating that our cooperative instincts extend far beyond kin selection.

Moreover, developmental psychology shows that human infants display empathy and helping behaviors before they're socialized to do so. This suggests cooperation precedes learned self-interest, not the other way around.

The neuroscience evidence is equally compelling. Brain imaging studies reveal that cooperative behavior activates reward centers automatically, while selfish acts require conscious effort to override our cooperative impulses. This contradicts the egoistic claim that selfishness is more "natural."

Some ethical egoists might argue that these cooperative tendencies still serve ultimate self-interest. However, this argument ignores the fundamental issue: if cooperation requires less cognitive effort than selfishness, then self-interest is learned behavior, not natural instinct.

Maybe in defending the "naturalness" of self-interest, ethical egoists have overlooked the evolutionary evidence. Instead of assuming selfishness is default, we should recognize that humans are naturally cooperative beings who must learn to be selfish.
