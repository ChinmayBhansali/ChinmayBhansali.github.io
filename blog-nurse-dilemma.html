Virtue Ethics and the Nurse's Dilemma

Is it morally justifiable to falsify medical documents to help a friend in desperate need?

February 10, 2026 Ethics Virtue Ethics
Prompt: A nurse's close friend is in desperate need of medical help but cannot afford treatment. Is it morally justifiable for the nurse to falsify medical documents to help their friend, even though it violates their professional duties?

From the perspective of virtue ethics, it is not morally justifiable for a nurse to falsify medical documents to help a friend, even in desperate circumstances, because such an action undermines essential professional virtues like honesty, integrity, and justice.

Virtue ethics emphasizes the development and exercise of moral character traits—virtues—such as honesty, compassion, integrity, and practical wisdom, especially in healthcare settings. These virtues guide professionals to act in ways that promote excellence and the good of patients and society, rather than simply following rules or seeking good outcomes. Honesty and integrity are repeatedly identified as foundational virtues for medical professionals, ensuring trust and fairness in patient care.

By falsifying documents, the nurse would be acting dishonestly and violating the trust placed in them by patients, colleagues, and society. This action not only risks harm to the healthcare system's integrity but also erodes the nurse's own moral character. Virtue ethics holds that repeated dishonest actions habituate vice rather than virtue, making it harder for the nurse to act rightly in future dilemmas. Furthermore, the nurse's professional role carries a responsibility to uphold justice and fairness for all patients, not just personal acquaintances.

One might argue that compassion—a key virtue in healthcare—could justify bending the rules to help someone in dire need. The nurse's desire to alleviate suffering and act benevolently toward their friend is understandable and reflects a caring disposition.

However, virtue ethics requires the integration of all relevant virtues, not just compassion. True moral excellence involves balancing compassion with honesty, integrity, and justice. Falsifying documents, even for a good cause, undermines these other virtues and sets a dangerous precedent, potentially harming others and the profession's reputation. A virtuous nurse would seek alternative, honest ways to help, such as advocating for financial assistance or connecting the friend to available resources, rather than resorting to deception.

In sum, virtue ethics does not justify falsifying medical documents, even out of compassion. The nurse should strive to embody all relevant virtues, seeking solutions that uphold honesty, integrity, and justice while still caring for those in need.
