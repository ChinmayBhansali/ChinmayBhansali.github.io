Is Using Your Neighbor's Wi-Fi Ethical?

Even if it causes no harm, is using an unsecured network without permission a reflection of poor character?

March 17, 2026 Ethics Virtue Ethics
Prompt: If your neighbour's wireless network extends into your home, is it ethically wrong for you to use the network to get free Internet access, provided you do not affect them directly?

From the perspective of Virtue Ethics, using a neighbor's unsecured wireless network without permission—even if it causes no direct harm—is ethically wrong because it reflects poor character traits such as dishonesty, disrespect, and lack of integrity.

Virtue Ethics emphasizes that "a good person does the right thing at the right time for the right reason". It focuses not just on the action or its consequences, but on the agent—the person performing the action—and whether their behavior reflects moral virtues. In this case, the action involves accessing a resource that belongs to someone else without their knowledge or consent. Even if the network is unsecured, the user is still benefiting from another's property without engaging in a relationship of trust or mutual respect.

Virtue Ethics teaches that moral decision-making cannot be reduced to rule-following or consequence-calculating alone. Instead, it asks whether the action reflects virtues like honesty, respect, and fairness, or vices like sneakiness, entitlement, or selfishness. Using someone's network without permission—even if it doesn't slow their connection or violate laws—fails to embody a virtuous person.

One might argue that if the network is open and the use causes no harm, the action is harmless and even practical—akin to enjoying music heard through a neighbor's open window.

However, unlike passive exposure, accessing a network is an intentional act that uses someone else's resources. Virtue Ethics would ask: What kind of person does this make me? A person of good character would seek permission, respect boundaries, and act transparently. The act of quietly benefiting from someone else's setup without acknowledgment reflects a failure to cultivate moral excellence.

Under Virtue Ethics, the action is unethical—not because of its consequences, but because it reflects a character lacking in respect and integrity. Ethical living involves more than avoiding harm; it requires actively embodying virtues that build trust and community.
