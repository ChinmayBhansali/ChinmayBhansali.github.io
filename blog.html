Ethical Frameworks

An ethical theory is considered "workable" if it can produce explanations that aim to persuade a skeptical but open-minded audience using good, rational arguments.

✕ Unworkable Ethical Frameworks

These theories are generally considered unworkable because they fail to provide universal or logical grounds for persuading others in a rigorous ethical debate.

Subjective Relativism

This framework asserts that there is no universal standard of right or wrong. Instead, every individual must decide for themselves what is moral. Its core defense is "Who are you to criticize my values?". While it contains a kernel of truth regarding the virtue of tolerance, it universalizes this tolerance to the point of absurdity.

Kernel of truth: Tolerance is a virtue, and individuals should not be dismissed for having different perspectives.

Cultural Relativism

This theory claims that moral actions are based entirely on a specific culture's actual moral guidelines. Because different cultures hold different moral standards, it is viewed as presumptuous to judge another culture's values.

Kernel of truth: Understanding other cultures can be difficult, and superficial judgments have historically been wrong.

Divine Command Theory

This framework dictates that good actions are those that God has willed. Therefore, moral decision-making should be guided by holy books (e.g., "Stealing is wrong, because the Bible says so").

Kernel of truth: Religious traditions contain much moral wisdom accumulated over centuries.

Ethical Egoism

This theory argues that an individual's only ethical obligation is their own self-interest. Actions are considered morally right if they advance one's own long-term interests, and helping others is only moral if it aligns with those personal interests.

Kernel of truth: Allowing people to follow their self-interest can sometimes yield better social outcomes than central planning.

✓ Workable Ethical Frameworks

These frameworks provide a more systematic, logical basis for determining right from wrong.

1.

Kantianism

Rooted in the philosophy of Immanuel Kant

The key goal of Kantianism is to derive morality from basic, rational principles. It proposes that the only thing in the world that is good without qualification is a "good will," or the desire to do the right thing.

  • First Formulation of the Categorical Imperative: You should act only from moral rules that you can simultaneously will to become universal moral laws without creating a logical contradiction.
  • Second Formulation of the Categorical Imperative: You should act in a way that treats both yourself and other people as ends in themselves, and never only as a means to an end.
  • It defines "perfect duties" (duties you are obliged to fulfill without exception, like telling the truth) and "imperfect duties" (duties like helping others, which are cause for praise but never fully "done").
2.

Utilitarianism

Conceived by Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill

A consequentialist theory where the morality of an action is based solely on its consequences, rather than the actor's intent. It evaluates the "utility" of an outcome, which is its tendency to produce happiness or prevent unhappiness.

  • Act Utilitarianism: An action is right or wrong to the extent that it increases or decreases the total happiness of the affected parties. You simply add up the change in happiness of all affected beings; if the sum is greater than zero, the action is good.
  • Rule Utilitarianism: Instead of looking at individual actions, this applies the Principle of Utility to moral rules. Society ought to adopt moral rules which, if followed by everyone, would lead to the greatest increase in total happiness.
3.

Social Contract Theory

Developed by Thomas Hobbes, Jean-Jacques Rousseau, and John Rawls

This theory posits that morality consists of the set of rules governing how people treat each other. Rational people will agree to accept these rules for their mutual benefit, provided that others follow them as well.

  • It operates heavily on the concepts of negative rights (rights guaranteed by others leaving you alone) and positive rights (rights obliging others to do something on your behalf).
  • Rawls's Principles of Justice: Everyone should have a fully adequate number of basic rights and liberties. Any social or economic inequalities must be to the greatest benefit of the least-advantaged members of society.
4.

Virtue Ethics

Tracing back to Aristotle

Virtue Ethics argues that true happiness and human flourishing lie in living a life of virtue. A right action is simply an action that a virtuous person, acting in character, would do in the same circumstances.

  • Virtues are character traits needed to flourish, and they are developed by habitually repeating virtuous actions (e.g., developing honesty by telling the truth).
  • A virtue typically lies right in the middle of two extreme vices (one of excess, and one of abstinence). For example, courage is the virtue that sits perfectly between the vice of cowardice (being too afraid to act) and the vice of rashness (acting without thinking).
