I built a prototype using AI to save time.

It was for a team course project. My team needed a mid-fidelity prototype and we were behind schedule. The AI delivered everything we asked for, cleanly, in minutes. I thought: this is what the future feels like.

But during our pilot session, our TA flagged something. The drag left a trail behind the finger. A thin, faint smear of motion that nobody had asked for. We all saw it. But none of us knew why it was there.

And that is the problem.

When you are not part of the thinking process, you cannot interrogate the result. The AI had made a design decision. It just had not told anyone. And we, the designers in the room, had no framework to even recognize it as a decision.

## Is this good or bad for design?

AI in product development promises speed, scale, and accessibility. Anyone can build an interface now. A developer can skip the designer entirely. A founder can ship a product in a weekend. The barrier to creation has never been lower.

But what about the barrier to understanding?

What happens when the people building interfaces have never been taught to ask why?

I argue that as AI lowers the barrier to building software, it raises the cost borne by users. Not through broken interfaces, but through misaligned ones. Tools that generate outputs based on statistical patterns do not apply UX principles by default. They produce what looks right, not what feels right. The resulting interfaces are not failures. They are invisible frictions, accumulated across millions of interactions, paid for by users who never knew they were being charged.

I call this the friction tax.

## The startup founder's question

A few days after the pilot session, I went to a design event. A student building a startup asked why he would ever pay a UX professional when AI could do the same thing faster and for free.

He is not wrong that AI can do it faster. He is wrong that faster is the same as right.

Some might argue that AI is merely a tool and that trained designers will always be in the room to catch what it misses. But this argument ignores the reality of who is actually building products today. It is not always trained designers. It is developers, entrepreneurs, and product managers making design decisions through prompt boxes, often without realizing they are making design decisions at all.

The friction does not come from malice. It comes from the absence of the question.

## What the trail was telling us

Malfunction is legible. When something breaks, you know it broke. You can point to it. You can fix it.

Misalignment is not. When an interface says something that was never intended to be said, nobody files a bug report. The user just feels a little worse about the experience and does not know why. They tap through it. They close the app. They do not come back.

The trail was misalignment. The AI had a theory about what a drag interaction should feel like, built from patterns in its training data, and it applied that theory without being asked and without explaining itself. The result looked fine. It was not fine. And none of us in that room, trained in design methods, conducting a proper usability pilot, caught it until someone who was not part of the building process looked at it fresh.

That is the gap I think we are not talking about enough. Not whether AI can design. It clearly can produce something that looks like design. But whether the people using AI to design have the vocabulary to know when the output is working against the user.

## The question worth asking

Before you ship the prototype the AI built in twenty minutes, ask this: do you know why every element looks the way it does?

Not can you describe it. Why it is there. What decision it encodes. What it is telling the user, intentionally or not.

If you cannot answer that, someone else will pay for it. Not you. The person using the product.

The trail is still there. We just have to learn to see it.
