My professor, Kevin, asked us a question I have not been able to put down since.

It was in an ethics course about technology and society. He described a hypothetical: a post-scarcity economy. A world where everyone has enough. Where the fundamental problem of survival has been solved. Then he asked: what would people do? What do we actually want to achieve as a society?

Nobody in the room had a clean answer. I do not think he expected one.

But the question changed something in how I think about what I build.

## The machine we forgot to question

Almost everything about modern professional life is organized around scarcity. The optimal career path. The salary benchmark. The visa that leads to the better visa. The startup that needs to scale. The product that needs to convert. These are not bad things. They are rational responses to a world where resources are limited and competition is real.

But they are answers to a specific question: how do I survive and thrive in this economy?

They are not answers to Kevin's question.

What do we actually want, when survival is no longer the answer?

I think most people in technology have never been asked to sit with that question seriously. Not because they are shallow, but because the work moves fast and the question moves slow. There is always a deadline, a sprint, a launch. The deeper question gets deferred until later, and later never comes.

But the question does not disappear. It just gets answered by default. By the metrics you optimize for. By the users you choose to center. By the features you build and the ones you cut. Every product decision is a values decision, whether you made it consciously or not.

## What this has to do with design

I study Human-Computer Interaction. I think about the gap between what systems produce and what people actually need. And the more I work in this space, the more I believe that the biggest design failures are not technical. They are philosophical.

They happen when a team optimizes for the metric without asking what the metric is for. When an interface is built to convert rather than to help. When AI is used to generate design decisions at speed without anyone asking what those decisions are communicating to the person on the other side.

The question is not whether the product works. The question is whether it is working toward something worth working toward.

That is Kevin's question, applied to UX design.

## I do not have an answer yet

I want to be honest about that. I do not know what a post-scarcity world looks like or whether we will ever build one. I do not know how to hold the tension between building things that sustain you financially and building things that you can be proud of. I am twenty-one years old and still figuring out what I actually believe.

But I think asking the question changes what you build. It changes who you build it for. It changes whether you are willing to slow down and ask why, even when slowing down costs something.

Most of the technology being built right now is being built to extract something from people. Attention. Money. Data. I do not want to build extraction mechanisms. I want to build things that would still make sense in that hypothetical world, where the only reason to make something is because it genuinely helps someone.

I do not know if I will always succeed at that. But I think it is worth trying to ask the question before the first line of code.

What would you build if survival wasn't the problem?
