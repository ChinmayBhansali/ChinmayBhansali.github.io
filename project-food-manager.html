HCI Research • CPSC 344

Food Manager

A human-centered design project exploring food management solutions through iterative design stages: from user research to mid-fidelity prototyping.

Role: Lead Researcher & Qualitative Data Analyst

Duration: 3 months

Team: 5 members

Design Process

We followed the User Interface Design Process framework, progressing through Pre-Design, Early Design, and Mid Design stages. Each stage built upon the previous, evolving from understanding users to creating testable prototypes.

Pre-Design

The Pre-Design stage focused on understanding our users: who they are and their key tasks. I led the user research effort, conducting the majority of interviews and performing qualitative data analysis to uncover design requirements.

Goals

Our primary goal was to understand the problem space of household food waste. We aimed to answer three research questions:

  • RQ1: How often do people buy excess groceries that are ultimately wasted?
  • RQ2: What type of food is wasted most in households and why?
  • RQ3: What approaches do people take to reduce their food waste?

Methods

We conducted semi-structured interviews with 11 participants from varied living situations (alone, with roommates, or with family). Participants ranged from ages 17–30, all recruited from CPSC 344. We recorded responses on datasheets and asked follow-up questions to probe deeper into their food waste habits.

After data collection, I performed thematic analysis using affinity diagramming to identify patterns across participant responses. This involved grouping related responses and synthesizing them into actionable themes.

Key Findings

Finding 1: Most people waste food monthly or less

Out of 11 participants, 7 reported throwing out groceries once a month or less, while 3 reported waste every 1–2 weeks, and 1 said they never wasted food. This suggests food waste is a common but not daily occurrence for most.

Finding 2: Short shelf-life items are wasted most

8 out of 11 participants reported that items with short shelf-life (vegetables, fruits, and prepared meals) were wasted most frequently. The primary reasons included quick expiration, buying more than needed, and simply forgetting about the food.

Finding 3: Four solution themes emerged

Participants' strategies for reducing food waste clustered into four themes: estimating food quantity, proper storage/preservation, conscious consumption, and tracking.

Products

The Pre-Design stage produced a comprehensive Design Requirements Report that included:

  • User scenarios representing different stakeholder needs
  • Task analyses breaking down how users currently manage food
  • Two central tasks our design needed to support:
    1. Track Food Inventory — keeping track of what food users have and when it expires
    2. Find Use Ideas — helping users find ways to use ingredients before they expire
  • Design requirements with measurable metrics for evaluation

Early Design

The Early Design stage focused on understanding the design space and choosing our approach. We developed conceptual models, created low-fidelity prototypes, and conducted cognitive walkthroughs to validate our direction.

Goals

Our goals were to explore the design space, identify risks, and choose a design approach that would address the two central tasks: tracking food inventory and finding use ideas before food expires.

Conceptual Model: "Conveyor" → "Factory"

Our initial "Conveyor" conceptual model imagined a conveyor belt where grocery items moved toward expiry. Items would be visible at all times, with their position representing time until expiration.

However, cognitive walkthroughs revealed this model couldn't connect with recipe-finding features. We evolved it into the "Factory" model, adding task-specific "stations":

  • Groceries tab = "Bulletin board" - requests items to purchase
  • Pantry tab = "Loading zone" - tracks items and expiry dates
  • Find Recipes = "Factory floor" - uses items before expiry

Methods

We used low-fidelity prototyping to quickly explore ideas and cognitive walkthroughs to evaluate them internally. Initial designs had five screens for distinct tasks, but internal evaluations found this too cluttered. We simplified to two main tabs (Grocery List and Pantry) with integrated features.

Products

The Early Design stage produced throw-away prototypes, a refined design direction, and the "Factory" conceptual model that would guide our medium-fidelity work.

Mid Design

In the Mid Design stage, I created the final linear Figma prototype for usability testing, conducted the majority of data collection sessions, and performed qualitative analysis on the results.

Goals

We aimed to evaluate our medium-fidelity prototype against three research questions:

  • Efficiency: How efficient is the design for logging items, checking expiry dates, and finding recipes?
  • Integration: How well could the design integrate into users' food management routines?
  • Visibility: How visible are relevant information and intended actions?

Methods

We conducted usability testing with 11 participants (ages 19+). Each session included think-aloud observation while completing tasks, followed by a questionnaire with Likert-scale and open-ended questions. Sessions were recorded on UBC Zoom when participants consented.

I tracked objective metrics (click counts, task completion) using coding sheets and performed thematic analysis on questionnaire responses to identify patterns in user feedback.

Final Prototype

Our final design is a mobile app combining a grocery list, inventory tracker, and recipe finder (tools most people are familiar with) to create positive transfer when adopting it into daily routines.

Key Findings

Finding 1: Logging food items is efficient; recipe search is not

Logging food items fell within our 3-click buffer from optimal, but searching for recipes averaged ~8.9 clicks (well outside the buffer). Questionnaire responses showed >80% non-negative ratings for efficiency on logging tasks.

Finding 2: Users felt the design was efficient despite objective data

Interestingly, participants reported feeling the design was efficient even when click counts were high. This discrepancy may be due to the novelty effect: users weren't shown how to use the prototype beforehand.

Finding 3: Good integration for list-makers, friction for others

6/11 participants agreed they would frequently use our design. However, 3 were undecided, citing the app as "too much work." The design integrates well for users who already track or itemize tasks but may introduce friction for general users.

Finding 4: Good visibility for core features, confusion on recipe search

Questionnaire data showed only 2/44 negative responses for visibility on grocery/pantry features. However, thematic analysis revealed recurring confusion about recipe search filters and the "check" completion buttons.

Products & Recommendations

The Mid Design stage produced a Design Outcome Report with validated findings and concrete recommendations for future iteration:

  1. Differentiate "mark as complete" button and add a "select" button
  2. Color the filters on "Find Recipes" and remove visual clutter
  3. Highlight the "Edit" button as the main method to select pantry items

Reflection

This project concluded after the Mid Design stage. While we didn't proceed to high-fidelity implementation, the work produced validated design requirements, a robust conceptual model, and actionable recommendations backed by user research.

Key learnings: In hindsight, we should have allowed users to explore the prototype before testing to avoid measuring learnability instead of efficiency. We also learned the importance of more low-fidelity iteration before progressing to medium-fidelity prototyping.
