title: "AI in the Courtroom: Justice or Bias Amplified?"
date: 2026-01-20
---
> **Prompt:** Pick a specific AI application described in [the article](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2021/07/21/ai-we-should-fear-is-already-here/) and argue either that it will be beneficial or harmful overall.

Daron Acemoglu, in the article "The AI we should fear is already here", makes some very valid points along with some points that are not so valid anymore. However, one of those still valid points interests me quite a bit.

Daron states that "[Narrow AI is] being used in the U.S. justice system for bail decisions and, now increasingly, in sentencing." And I cannot agree more on this. However, is this good or bad for the society? AI in courtrooms promises objective judgement without biases. But what about the deeper aspects of this AI, such as the data it has been trained on or the reasoning it does?

I argue that despite all of its promises, AI worsens the human errors that already persists in the decision-making inside a courtroom.

COMPAS is a popular algorithm that is used in judgement in the US courts. It has been trained on the past proceedings of the American courts, and it is supposed to remove human bias based on race or class. However, the ProPublica COMPAS study in 2016 found out that COMPAS was consistently marking black people as high risk whereas it marked white people very leniently even though they went on to redo those crimes. This study, along with many other examples, show how the AI, instead of removing human biases, is reinstating the biases of the courtroom proceedings from the past decade.

Another major drawback of using AI in courtroom decision-making is the lack of transparency. Most of these AI used in the courtrooms are being made by private entities, and these private entities refuse to show the codebase or even the weightage of the variables used in the model training of those AIs. And even if we know about the codebase, the bare issue of the "black box" reasoning of an AI makes it impossible to challenge its reasoning. This fundamentally violates the human right to challenge any evidence or reasoning used against them.

Some proponent visionaries might argue that the AI is supposed to be used as a tool in making decisions instead of depending totally on it. However, this argument ignores the fundamental psychological impact of having a high risk score on a person by AI on the final decision-making of the judge.

Maybe in the search of a statistical solution to errors in human judgement, we have forgotten the very essence of law, which is to help people with empathy and morality; the two aspects that a narrow AI can never achieve. Instead of outsourcing our decision making, we should search on ways to improve human judgement.
