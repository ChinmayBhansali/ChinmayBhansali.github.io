---
title: "Loyalty Cards: Fair Pricing or Data Coercion?"
date: 2026-03-03
framework: Kantianism
---
> **Prompt:** Critics of grocery club cards give examples of card-member prices being equal to the regular product price at stores without customer loyalty programs. Is it fair for a store to charge us more if we don't want to use its loyalty card?

It is unfair for a store to charge customers more for not using a loyalty card, as this practice violates the Kantian ethical principle of treating individuals as ends in themselves rather than merely as means to a business goal.

Consider the case of Safeway's Club Card program, where advertised discounts are only available to cardholders. A 2003 San Francisco Chronicle investigation found that many "discounted" prices were equivalent to regular prices at stores without loyalty programs. Non-cardholders, therefore, paid more for the same goods, not because of any added value, but because they declined to share personal data.

Kantianism emphasizes that moral actions must respect the autonomy and dignity of individuals. By coercing customers into surrendering personal information to avoid inflated prices, stores treat them as mere data sources (means to an end) rather than as rational agents capable of making informed choices. The loyalty card system, in this context, is not about rewarding loyalty but about extracting value from consumer data under the guise of discounts.

Supporters of loyalty programs argue that they are voluntary and offer benefits to those who opt in. Stores use the data to improve inventory, personalize offers, and enhance customer experience.

While the programs are technically optional, the pricing structure creates a coercive environment. Customers who choose not to participate are penalized financially, undermining the voluntariness of the choice. This violates the Kantian imperative that moral rules must be universalizable; if every store adopted this model, consumers would be forced to trade privacy for fair pricing, which is ethically untenable.

Under Kantian ethics, the practice of charging higher prices to non-loyalty card users is unjust. It fails to respect consumer autonomy and treats individuals as tools for data collection. Ethical business practices should offer genuine rewards for loyalty, not impose penalties for privacy.
