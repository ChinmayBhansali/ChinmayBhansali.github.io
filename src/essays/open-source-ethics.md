title: "Open Source and Commercial Ethics"
date: 2026-02-24
framework: Kantianism
---
> **Prompt:** A business-minded undergraduate student copies open-source academic software, makes small improvements, and launches an edtech startup selling the system to different universities. Is it ethical for the student to build and sell this commercial product based on software created for educational research purposes?

According to Kantian ethics, it is unethical for the student to commercialize the educational software without consent, because the action fails the test of universalizability and treats the original creators merely as a means to an end.

Kantianism is grounded in the Categorical Imperative, which demands that we act only according to maxims we can will to become universal laws. In this case, the student's maxim might be: "It is acceptable to take open-source academic software, make minor changes, and sell it for profit." If universally adopted, this would undermine academic collaboration and discourage researchers from sharing tools openly. A real-world parallel is the controversy around companies like Clearview AI, which scraped public images for commercial facial recognition without consent, raising ethical and legal concerns.

Kant emphasizes respect for persons; each individual must be treated as an end in themselves, not merely as a means. The graduate students and faculty who developed the software intended it for research and education, not profit. By repurposing their work for commercial gain without acknowledgment or permission, the undergraduate student disregards their autonomy and intentions, using their labor as a stepping stone for personal benefit.

One might argue that the software was open-source, and thus legally available for modification and redistribution, including commercial use. The student simply recognized an opportunity and acted within the bounds of the license.

Kantian ethics is not based on legality but on moral duty. Even if the license permits commercial use, the ethical question hinges on intent and respect. The student's actions lack integrity if they ignore the original purpose and contributors. Ethical behavior would involve seeking collaboration, crediting the original developers, and ensuring the commercialization aligns with the spirit of the project.

From a Kantian perspective, the student's commercialization of the educational software is unethical. It fails the test of universalizability and disrespects the original creators by treating their work as a mere tool for profit. Ethical innovation must honor the intentions and dignity of all contributors.
