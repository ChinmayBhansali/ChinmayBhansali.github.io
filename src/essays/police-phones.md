> **Prompt:** The police should be allowed to ask you to unlock and hand over your phone if they first tell you that you're not legally required to give it to them.

Under Social Contract Theory, the police should not be allowed to ask individuals to unlock and hand over their phones, even if they clarify that it is not legally required, because such requests undermine the mutual trust and fairness that form the foundation of a just society.

A relevant case is R v. Fearon (2014), where the Supreme Court of Canada ruled that police may search a suspect's cellphone incident to arrest, but only under strict conditions. The decision emphasized the need for balancing law enforcement interests with privacy rights, acknowledging that cellphones contain vast personal information. This case illustrates the tension between state authority and individual rights, a central concern of Social Contract Theory.

Social Contract Theory posits that individuals consent to surrender certain freedoms in exchange for protection and order provided by the state. However, this consent is conditional: the state must uphold justice, fairness, and the rights of its citizens. When police ask individuals to unlock their phones without legal compulsion, they exploit the power imbalance inherent in law enforcement encounters. This undermines the voluntary nature of the social contract, as individuals may feel coerced into compliance despite being told they are not legally obligated.

Moreover, the expectation of privacy in one's personal devices is a modern extension of the right to personal autonomy. The social contract does not grant the state unchecked access to private information; rather, it requires that such access be justified, proportionate, and legally sanctioned.

Proponents might argue that voluntary cooperation with police, even when not legally required, enhances public safety and expedites justice. In this view, the social contract includes a duty to assist law enforcement in maintaining order.

However, the legitimacy of the social contract depends on informed and voluntary consent. In high-pressure situations, individuals may not feel free to refuse a police request, especially when unaware of their rights. This coerced compliance violates the principle of fairness and erodes public trust in law enforcement. A just society must ensure that citizens are protected from undue pressure and that their rights are respected, even in the pursuit of security.

Social Contract Theory emphasizes mutual respect and fairness between the state and its citizens. Allowing police to request access to personal devices without legal compulsion breaches this ethical framework by fostering coercion and undermining informed consent. To preserve the integrity of the social contract and uphold individual rights, such requests should not be permitted.
