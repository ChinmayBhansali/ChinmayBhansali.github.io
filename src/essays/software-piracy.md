---
title: "Software Piracy and Intellectual Property"
date: 2026-03-24
framework: Rule Utilitarianism
---
> **Prompt:** At your workplace, you discover that a key piece of software being used is an unlicensed, bootlegged copy. When you raise the issue with your boss, they explain that the licensed version is too expensive and promise to purchase it later when the company's finances improve. Should the company keep using the software under these circumstances?

The company should not continue using unlicensed software, even temporarily, because doing so undermines intellectual property rights, creates unfair market conditions, and violates ethical principles that sustain trust and innovation.

Rule Utilitarianism holds that we should adopt moral rules whose universal application maximizes overall happiness. A rule permitting businesses to use pirated software when finances are tight would erode the software industry's incentive to innovate, harming developers and consumers long-term. Intellectual property laws exist to overcome barriers to entry, the high upfront costs of software development, by granting creators exclusive rights to recoup investments. If piracy becomes normalized, these barriers increase because firms must invest more in anti-piracy measures, raising costs and reducing competition.

A real-world example illustrates this risk: About three-quarters of Microsoft product users in China did not have properly licensed software, prompting Microsoft in 2015 to offer free upgrades to Windows 10 for all users, including those with pirated copies (Quinn, 2024). This shows how widespread infringement distorts pricing strategies and undermines sustainable business models.

Universalizing the rule "Use pirated software until you can afford a license" would incentivize widespread infringement, reducing profits for developers and discouraging new entrants. This harms the software ecosystem, which thrives on fair competition and innovation. While the short-term benefit to the company is cost savings, the long-term harm includes legal risks, reputational damage, and diminished trust in professional ethics.

One might argue that the company's survival justifies temporary infringement, as bankruptcy would harm employees and clients. From an economic perspective, high licensing costs act as a **barrier to entry** for small firms, limiting their ability to compete.

Rule Utilitarianism rejects exceptions that undermine universal rules. Allowing piracy for financial hardship creates a slippery slope where firms routinely bypass legal obligations. Alternatives exist, such as open-source software or subscription models, that reduce barriers without violating rights. These options preserve fairness and promote innovation without sacrificing ethical integrity.

Under Rule Utilitarianism, the moral rule "Respect intellectual property rights" produces the greatest long-term good by sustaining innovation, reducing barriers to entry through legitimate competition, and maintaining trust in professional conduct. Therefore, the company should cease using the bootlegged software and seek lawful alternatives immediately.
