Personal Essay

Who Am I?

There is only one plot in every fiction: Who am I?

I have been asking that question longer than I knew I was asking it.

I grew up in multiple schools in Rajasthan, India, where electronics were banned entirely. No laptops, no phones, no screens. Just notebooks, pens, and the long supervised quiet of a system that had very clear ideas about what a good student looked like. He studied when others played, answered correctly when called upon, and did not speak unprompted. He kept his curiosity to himself, earned the highest marks in his class, and learned very early that the safest thing a curious mind can do is stay hidden. I was that student, and I was good at it — maybe too good.

But some part of us resists being fully shaped. Even in the silence the system required, something kept leaking through. When my friends and I wanted to play games and had nothing to play them on, I didn't feel the restriction of technology as a wall; I felt it as an invitation. I designed a Pokémon RPG on notebook paper: hand-drawn maps, turn-based battles tracked in pencil, rules written in the margins. My friends played it and got lost in it. I remember watching them more than I played it myself, watching the way their faces changed when the story surprised them, wondering without words what had just passed between my mind and theirs through nothing but paper. I didn't have a name for what that was, but I just knew I needed to keep doing it.

I arrived in Canada at seventeen with a scholarship, two suitcases, and no laptop. A store employee sold me one I thought I understood. Two years later, I started narrowing down on what I wanted to learn and realized that my laptop didn't suit my needs anymore. When I finally started researching why, I fell into something unexpected: the discovery that people at companies like Apple and Microsoft make deliberate, considered choices about who their users are and what those users need. I realized that design is not decoration; someone, somewhere, is deciding how millions of people experience a machine. And some of the time, those decisions get made without the people in mind at all. That was the first time I understood what I wanted to study, not because it was a smart career move, but because it was the same question I had been living since the notebook RPG: what passes between a mind and a system, and how do we make that passage more honest? Human-Computer Interaction gave my oldest curiosity its first real name.

But I need to be honest about something. A story that only shows the insight and hides the struggle is not a story — it's a performance. And I have been performing long enough. I arrived at UBC still wearing a version of myself that had been shaped almost entirely by what others expected: the accent I used, the opinions I held, the way I moved through rooms. I adapted everything, constantly, like someone who has forgotten that adaptation was once a survival strategy and not a personality. I was so good at becoming what an environment needed that I sometimes lost track of who I was underneath it all. There is a very thin line between trust and obligation, and for a long time, I had been fulfilling obligations I had never consciously agreed to: performing the good student, the quiet one, the one who doesn't take up too much space.

While I was busy adapting to fit every room I entered, there was one core truth I could not adapt away from. What stayed constant through every environment and every version of myself I tried on was this: I could not watch someone suffer and do nothing. Not because I was trained to help, but because I just couldn't look away. When a teammate once hit a wall, exhausted, quietly falling apart under the weight of leading a project, I didn't announce anything or ask for credit. I just absorbed the work, doing mine and hers both. The idea that every human being has the capacity for theory of mind — the ability to step inside another person's experience and feel its weight — and that we should use it, is not something I arrived at through philosophy coursework. This is something I was born knowing, suppressed for years under the demands of a system that didn't reward it, and have been slowly, carefully excavating back into the light. UBC did not give me this, but it gave me somewhere safe enough to finally show it.

I have made choices that have cost me. I watch people fabricate entire projects on their resumes, paste in work they never did, and get callbacks I don't get. I know the system is being cheated, I know it is working, and I know I won't do it. Not because I'm naive about how the world operates, but because I refuse to optimize for a broken evaluator. My entire research life is about closing the gap between what systems assume about people and what people actually are. I cannot build that work on a foundation of pretending to be something I'm not. "Put up a fake front," I've been told by people who love me and want me safe. "Not everyone needs to know your real self." And maybe they're right about some rooms. But I keep thinking about the notebook RPG, about the faces of my friends when the story surprised them, and about what passed between us that wasn't performance or strategy. It was just genuine contact between one person's imagination and another's. That is what I want to spend my life building, and I cannot build it dishonestly.

My father studied for nine years after high school. He built everything from nothing. When he speaks about the world, he speaks from a track record I can see and touch and measure. I am speaking from potential — from a future that hasn't happened yet. And potential always sounds quieter than proof, even when it's pointing somewhere truer. So I go quiet sometimes, in rooms, in conversations, waiting until I know enough to earn the right to speak. Waiting for a completeness that never arrives. The irony is not lost on me: the very quality that might someday make my words worth hearing is the same one currently keeping me silent. I spent years cutting the thread between who I was required to be and who I actually am. The knots are there, and I can feel them, but I have started to think the knots might be the strongest parts.

There is a version of myself I can see clearly, ten years from now. He walks into rooms differently, not because he finally has enough proof or enough credentials, but because he stopped waiting for permission to take up space. His words carry weight not because he performs certainty, but because he has stopped pretending he doesn't have something to say. My dream is not a job title or a publication record. It is this: I want to understand, with rigor and with care, how humans and technology actually talk to each other. Not how we assume they do, and not how we design them to in ideal conditions. I want to know how they actually, honestly, imperfectly make contact, and how we close the gap when they don't. I want to build the infrastructure for empathy between people and the systems they inhabit. I have been living that question since I was a child with a notebook, a group of friends, and nothing but imagination and a good story between us.
